Quantum Annealing by Hidetoshi Nishimori

Executive Summary

  • Quantum annealing is a quantum-mechanical paradigm to solve combinatorial optimization problems and was proposed by Kadowaki and Nishimori in 1998.

  • Many problems in machine learning and artificial intelligence can be formulated as combinatorial optimization, and the development of efficient methods to solve combinatorial optimization problems has enormous practical significance.

  • A Canadian venture D-Wave Systems has developed a hardware to realize quantum annealing1). Google, NASA, and Lockheed-Martin in collaboration with USC are among their customers.　Los Alamos National Laboratory, evaluating quantum annealing as a "game-changing idea", also introduces a D-Wave machine.

  • Researchers discussed, for some time, if the D-Wave machine operates quantum mechanically. The argument has almost settled positively.

  • Researchers are discussing if the D-Wave machine can solve optimization problems more quickly than ordinary computers do. The answer depends on the problem and criteria of speed. See here for the list of recent scientific papers on this and other problems of quantum annealing.

  • Some people question whether the D-Wave machine can be called a quantum computer. One of the reasons of confusion is the ambiguity in the definition of quantum computer.

  • Development of circuit-model quantum computers (quantum computers in the conventional sense) lags far behind that of quantum-annealing machines.

  • The speed of circuit-model quantum computers far exceeds that of usual (classical) computers only for a limited number of problems, e.g., integer factoring, quantum simulation, and data-base search. Although circuit-model quantum computers are universal because they can process any computational tasks, the speed does not exceed that of classical computers except for the above-mentioned very special set of problems.

  • See also my Google TechTalk.

  • IARPA has started a program QEO (quantum enhanced optimization) to build a prototype quantum annealer with high performance.

Introduction

Quantum annealing is a computational paradigm to search for the minimum of a cost function (multivariable function to be minimized) through a control of quantum fluctuations. Quantum annealing is used mainly for combinatorial optimization problems with discrete variables. Many practically important problems can be formulated as combinatorial optimization, typically machine learning for pattern recognition, natural language processing, medical diagnosis, etc. Finding efficient methods to solve combinatorial optimization problems is therefore very important, and this is one of the reasons why quantum annealing attracts much attention.

To perform quantum annealing, one writes the cost function in terms of the Ising model of statistical mechanics. The Hamiltonian (energy function) of the Ising model should be chosen such that its lowest-energy state (ground state) represents the solution to the combinatorial optimization problem. A term representing quantum-mechanical fluctuations is then added to the Hamiltonian to induce quantum transitions between the states.

One first chooses a very large coefficient for the quantum term, which leads to to a uniform probability of the existence of states over all states as in the left panel (blue line) of the figure. This is a trivial state and can be realized easily. Then one gradually decreases the value of the coefficient of the quantum term. The state of the system follows the time-dependent Schrödinger equation, the natural time evolution of a physical system. The coefficient of the quantum term finally reaches zero, and only the classical term of the Hamiltonian, the Ising model whose ground state is to be identified, remains at the end of the process. It is then expected that the probability has by far the largest value for the solution (the right-most panel of the figure) to the combinatorial optimization problem at the end of the annealing process.

The system follows the Schrödinger equation to reach the final state on the right, in which the probability focuses on the solution to the combinatorial optimization problem. The abscissa represents the classical state, and the ordinate is for the value of the cost function or the classical Ising Hamiltonian to be minimized (black) and the quantum-mechanical probability of states (blue).

Historical note
This prescription of quantum annealing as widely used now was first proposed in 1998 [1], where the method was shown to be very effective for several combinatorial optimization problems. An experiment for a magnetic system soon followed [2] as well as large-scale numerical studies for complex systems [3]. Notice that Kadowaki had already done in his Thesis [4] something similar to [3]. Also, a continuous-space formulation, different from the Ising-model formulation for combinatorial optimization, had appeared in [5]. Quantum annealing is sometimes understood as a "noisy version" of quantum adiabatic computation in the sense that the former allows for environmental noise in contrast to the latter. A careful scrutiny of the above-listed papers reveals that these two ideas are essentially the same, at least in their initially-proposed formulations. Nevertheless, they differ in several aspects including the strict imposition of adiabaticity in quantum adiabatic computation. Quantum annealing, in contrast, allows for a non-adiabatic time evolution with the expectation that the system reaches the target state with a non-vanishing probability even without the adiabatic condition. Notice that noise is not intrinsic in quantum annealing, although it is not excluded. Also, references [2,3,6] all cite [1] as their predecessors. The origin of current high activities in this field is attributed to [1].

Strengths and Weaknesses

Quantum annealing is a type of quantum computation because it uses quantum effects in the process to solve combinatorial optimization problems. Let us compare quantum annealing with a few related methods.

  • Simulated annealing

    Simulated annealing is a generic method to solve optimization problems using classical probability, or thermal effects in analogy with statistical mechanics, and is used widely in practical applications. It has been shown that quantum annealing, in theory, allows the system to approach the correct solution more quickly than simulated annealing if we control the coefficient of the transverse field Γ(t) as a function of time t in the same manner as the temperature T(t) in simulated time [7]. Quantum annealing can be simulated on a conventional computer using quantum Monte Carlo simulations. Dedicated quantum devices have the potential to perform quantum annealing much more efficiently and are tested extensively.

    [7] S. Morita and H. Nishimori, "Mathematical foundation of quantum annealing", .J. Math. Phys. 49 (2008) 125210.

  • Circuit model

    Many of the studies of quantum computation concern the circuit (gate) model, in which one applies quantum gates one by one to the state of a system toward the desired solution of the problem. A large number of research papers have been published, but experiments have been seriously plagued by decoherence, i.e., destruction of quantum states through interactions with the environment. It seems still very long before a device of real-life usefulness is built. Also, the application of the circuit-model quantum computer is very limited: e.g., integer factoring, simulations of quantum systems, and solving linear equations show essential speedup by circuit-model quantum computation. For other problems, circuit-model quantum computers are not fast.　In other words, a circuit-model quantum computer, if ever realized, is an extremely special-purpose machine.

    A realistic application of circuit model quantum computers is quantum simulation of small-size molecules [8], the commercial value of which is not necessarily clear at this moment. As for the number factoring and Shor's algorithm, it is important to keep in mind that research efforts to jeopardize widely-used RSA cryptography should take into account legal and moral aspects. Also, methods to efficiently solve linear equations by circuit model quantum computers may be applied to machine learning and related tasks [9,10].

    [8] B. Bauer et al, "Hybrid quantum-classical approach to correlated materials", arXiv:1510.03859.
    [9] A. W. Harrow, A. Hassidim, and S. Lloyd, "Quantum algorithm for linear systems of equations", Phys. Rev. Lett. 103, 150502 (2009); Z. Li et al, "Experimental realization fo a quantum support vector machine", Phys. Rev. Lett. 114, 140504 (2015)
    [10] S. Aaronson, "Read the fine print", Nature Physics, 11, 291 (2015).

    In contrast, a quantum annealing machine can be used for combinatorial optimization, which has a wide range of practical applications. Also, integer-factoring algorithms for quantum annealing have been developed [11], but there is no guarantee that these algorithms are exponentially fast. It is known that quantum annealing, in its quantum adiabatic formulation, is theoretically equivalent to the gate model [12].

    [11] N. Xu et al, "Quantum Factorization of 143 on a Dipolar-Coupling Nuclear Magnetic Resonance System", Phys. Rev. Lett. 108 (2012) 130501; X. Peng et al, "Quantum Adiabatic Algorithm for Factorization and Its Experimental Implementation", Phys. Rev. Lett. 101 (2008) 220405.
    [12] D. Aharonov, W. van Dam, J. Kempe, Z. Landau, S. Lloyd, and O. Regev, "Adiabatic Quantum Computation is Equivalent to Standard Quantum Computation", SIAM J. Comp. 37 (2007) 166.

    Quantum annealing is less prone to decoherence than the circuit model since the former follows the inherently-stable ground state or low excited states. It is also an advantage of quantum annealing that one does not have to specify the time evolution explicitly: In quantum annealing, the system evolves literally naturally following the Schrödinger equation.

  • Classical (conventional) computer

    The goal of quantum annealing is to solve optimization problems. Quantum annealing machines are not to replace conventional computers that are useful for any purposes (universal computation). This fact is shared by the circuit model. One should not misunderstand that conventional computers will eventually be replaced by ultra-fast quantum computers in our daily applications.

    As long as optimization problems are concerned, there exists some evidence that quantum annealing outperforms simulated annealing carried out on classical computers. It is nevertheless unclear whether or not there exist problems of wide practical impact where quantum annealing achieves overwhelming (exponential) speedup over conventional methods. Notice that there indeed exist a problem in which an exponential speedup by quantum annealing (not by adiabatic quantum computation) is proved although this problem is of limited practical usefulness.

  • Computational complexity and related topics

    • Quantum annealing needs an exponentially long time to find the exact solution to the most difficult problems among all QUBO's (Ising models).

    • Indeed, it is explicitly shown from the analysis of energy spectra that quantum annealing spends an exponentially long time to solve several specific problems

    • It has nevertheless been well known from a number of numerical and analytical studies that quantum annealing improves the performance, if not exponentially, of solving many combinatorial optimization problems in comparison with classical simulated annealing.

    • It is not expected that quantum annealing would drastically speedup the time to reach solutions to many problems for which only exponentially slow classical algorithms are known. It is also incorrect, on the other hand, that there would exist no such cases. Indeed this paper shows that an exponential speedup is reached for the binary-tree problem. A small downside of this result is that the problem is academic and it is not clear how (and if) this result may have a social impact.

    • One of the important advantages of quantum annealing over the circuit model is that quantum annealing does not need an explicit construction of step-by-step instructions (algorithm) to run the process.

    • It is not necessary for quantum annealing to trace the instantaneous ground state. Evaluation of computational complexity based on the energy-gap analysis reveals only part of the possibilities of quantum annealing. Indeed, the above-mentioned solution to the binary-tree problem does not follow the instantaneous ground state. In other words, adiabatic quantum computation (AQC) is a subset of quantum annealing (QA) but the converse is not true. Also, quantum annealing may be understood to allow for interactions with the environment including temperature effects.

D-Wave machine and related topics

D-Wave Systems Inc. has developed a device that realizes quantum annealing at the hardware level and has installed a few of them at corporate users. The current version D-Wave 2X has more than 1,000 working qubits. Many scientific papers have appeared in which researchers have checked the performance of the machine or have solved prototypes combinatorial optimization problems. Data in these papers seem to indicate that quantum annealing is indeed realized in the machine. Further developments are worth careful attention.

IARPA is going to run a program QEO (Quantum-Enhanced Optimization). The goal is to build a prototype quantum annealer of about 100 qubits with high-performance superconducting qubits and with more complex connectivity between qubits.
